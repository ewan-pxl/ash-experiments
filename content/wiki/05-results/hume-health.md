# Hume Health: buy box rebuilds before BFCM

Three conversion-page redesigns, grounded in thousands of customer surveys, shipped a week before the peak.

Hume Health sells connected body-composition hardware (the Hume Pod smart scale and the Hume Band wearable). They came to us on October 6 with seven weeks until Black Friday. Instead of safe surface-level tests, we spent week one on research, then rebuilt the buy boxes on the three highest-traffic conversion pages (`/pages/pdp3`, `/pages/hume-body-pod`, `/pages/hume-band`) and got them live in early November, before the traffic surge hit.

## The hypothesis
Research came first. Week one was roughly 2,000 Pod surveys, 1,000 Band surveys, and 1,600+ live on-site insights. Two barriers came back loud and consistent:

- **Pod:** skepticism that it was "just another smart scale" and whether it justified the premium. People plateauing on the scale wanted proof beyond weight that fat was dropping and muscle was building.
- **Band:** price skepticism plus doubt that the data would be accurate enough to trust. Buyers were exhausted from "doing everything right" without seeing results.

So the bet was that rebuilding the buy boxes (including the product hero galleries) around three things would move conversion: credibility (science-backed accuracy claims), clarity (showing what is actually changing in your body), and proof of progress (data that validates the effort). Not more traffic, not deeper discounts. Fix the specific things stopping the purchase, before peak.

## What changed
- Recreated the buy box sections and product hero galleries on all three conversion pages.
- Rebuilt the messaging hierarchy around credibility, clarity, and proof of progress.
- Excluded traffic segments that diluted the read (for example influencer traffic) and optimised in real time toward the segments that actually convert.

## The result
Two phases, and they are not the same kind of number. Be careful repeating these.

**BFCM peak (Nov 24 to Dec 8, 14 days). Measured.**

| Product | CVR increase | Additional orders | Incremental revenue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hume Pod | +16.4% | 11,142 | $2,175,613 |
| Hume Band | +19.73% | 3,492 | $803,166 |
| Total | n/a | 14,634 | $2,978,779 |

**Post-BFCM (Dec 9 to Jan 31, 55 days). Projected, not measured.** The source labels this a projected result: 42,390 additional orders and $8,728,290 incremental revenue, at roughly $205.90 per order.

Combined across the 69 days the case study reports 57,024 incremental orders and $11,707,069. The headline "nearly $12M" is real but it is **14 days of measured BFCM lift plus 55 days of projection**. When you use it, say which is which.

## Why it worked
The lift came from removing the exact friction the surveys surfaced, on the pages paid traffic actually lands on, timed so the rebuilds were live before the highest-volume window of the year. Research told us what to build; the BFCM timing multiplied the impact; the real-time segment exclusion stopped us spending conversions on traffic that was never going to buy.

## The reusable insight
- **Research before build, not after.** Thousands of customer voices pointed at two specific barriers per product. We rebuilt around those, not around generic best practice.
- **The single biggest lever before a peak is timing.** A good Q4 versus a transformational one was not more spend, it was having the higher-converting page live before the surge rather than during it.
- **Exclude diluting traffic.** Cutting segments that do not convert (influencer traffic here) sharpens both the result and the read.
- **Hardware with a premium price converts on credibility and proof, not discounts.** For considered, higher-AOV products, "here is proof it works and that the data is trustworthy" beat price-led messaging.

## Other Hume wins worth keeping
Beyond the buy box case study, two patterns from the ongoing program are worth reusing:

- **Coupon Activation (test 033)** was a clear, statistically confident win. At ~155K visitors per arm: CVR +5.3% at 97.2% probability to beat control, cart abandonment down 2.9% at 99.8%, checkout abandonment down 3.4% at 99.9%. RPV was roughly flat (+3.0% at 77.8%) because the variant drove ~2x units per order at a lower price per unit. The team estimated 680+ incremental monthly orders and ~$56K incremental monthly revenue, and rolled it out starting on the Band page. Lesson: a well-placed coupon does its real work at the bottom of the funnel, closing intent that already exists. The inverted re-test (046, coupon removed) confirmed it: removing the coupon lifted ATC +17.6% and checkout-begin +19.3% at 99% confidence but killed the close and dropped revenue.
- **BOGO tests (034, 035)** lifted AOV hard (+24% to +25%) but dragged CVR (around -14%), so net RPV moved only single digits and off the back of order value, not conversion. Lesson: BOGO is an AOV lever, not a conversion lever. Read it on RPV, not CVR, and expect the gain to come from basket size.
