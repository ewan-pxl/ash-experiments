# Copy Swipefile

How we capture the words of customer per brand, and the structure every swipe file follows.

A swipe file here is not a folder of competitor screenshots. It's the brand's own customers
talking, captured verbatim and sorted so it's usable in copy. The principle: when a headline
uses the exact phrase a customer already said, the reader feels like they're having a
conversation with themselves. That's the bar. Every swipe file is per-client and lives in that
client's workspace; this doc is the template and the discipline behind it.

## Where the words come from

Open-ended (qualitative) customer surveys are the main source, run through the survey-analysis
prompt (see `prompt-library.md`). Reviews, support tickets, and call notes feed it too. We
cluster everything into three lenses:

- **Problem.** Life before the product. What was going on, what was broken.
- **Outcome.** What they wanted out of it. The change they were buying.
- **Friction.** What nearly stopped them, pre and post-purchase. The doubts, the cost worry,
  the setup fear.

## The four buckets in every swipe file

Beyond the problem/outcome/friction clustering, we pull raw language into four buckets. These
are what copy actually reaches for.

- **Slang and shorthand.** The category words customers use among themselves. Signals you know
  the world they live in.
- **Emotional phrases and analogies.** How they describe the feeling ("like Christmas morning
  every month", "a coach in your bathroom"). These become hero lines.
- **Practical shorthand and gripes.** The blunt complaints and value framings ("I'd walk out of
  Bass Pro with eighty bucks easy", "just another smart scale"). These become objection-handling
  copy and us-vs-them.
- **Voice and tone markers.** Regional words, register, the way they actually talk ("y'all",
  "dang", "no BS"). These calibrate the brand's voice so copy doesn't read corporate.

## What good looks like (a real example)

From the Mystery Tackle Box swipe file, the emotional bucket:

- "Like Christmas morning every month"
- "Fruit of the month club for fishing tackle"
- "Gear that pushed me out of my old style"
- "Fishing feels brand new again"

And the gripes bucket, which is where objection copy comes from:

- "Too many plastics, not enough hardbaits"
- "Don't send clearance junk"
- "I don't want duplicates"
- "Full-sized baits, not samples"

From the Hume Body Pod survey, the same buckets in a premium-health register:

- "It's like a DEXA scan you can do every day at home" (analogy)
- "A coach in your bathroom" (analogy)
- "Just another smart scale" (the core objection, repeated)
- "Don't sell me dreams. Show me data that makes sense." (voice marker)

Two completely different brands, same method: capture the phrase, tag the bucket, reach for it
when you write.

## Rules for capturing

- Verbatim where it's tactile. Paraphrase only to tighten, never to sanitise.
- Skip the generic ("I love it", "great product"). It's filler and it doesn't convert.
- Keep counts. Knowing a theme was cited 412 times vs 54 tells you what to lead with.
- Tag every captured line by lens (problem / outcome / friction) and by bucket.
