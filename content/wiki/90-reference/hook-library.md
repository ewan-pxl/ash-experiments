# Hook & Headline Library

Reusable headline shapes and the rules we hold them to, for landing pages, email, and ads.

These are scaffolding, not finished copy. The job is to take the words of customer from the
brand's swipe file (see `swipefile.md`) and pour them into a shape that's already proven to
carry attention. Pick a shape, fill the blanks with the customer's own language, then read it
aloud. If it sounds like a brochure, it's wrong.

## The house rules for a headline

These hold across every brand and every formula below. They come from the survey-to-copy
formulas the team runs.

- No product name in the headline. Sell the change, not the SKU.
- Never use a hyphen or a colon as a connector. Use a full stop or a comma.
- 50 to 100 characters for a headline. 60 to 120 per bullet. 160 to 240 for a paragraph.
- One headline per line, no numbering, no prefixes or suffixes.
- Match the brand's existing tone of voice, not ours.

The bank we reverse-engineer survey insight into. Each is a slot you fill with the customer's
problem, outcome, or aspiration.

- The truth about [desired action] (even in [difficult situation])

## Worked hooks (from a real swipe file)

These are finished headlines from the Mystery Tackle Box survey work, tagged by which lens
they pull on. They show the formulas filled with words of customer, not invented copy. Use them
as a calibration bar, not as copy to reuse.

- "Stop walking out of Bass Pro with two lures for eighty bucks." *(problem, outcome)*
- "Christmas morning shows up in your mailbox every month." *(outcome)*
- "Not another pack of plastics. Fresh gear, every time." *(friction, problem)*
- "Discover baits you'd never buy yourself." *(outcome)*
- "No clearance junk. Only gear you'd actually fish." *(friction)*
- "Pause, skip, or double up. Your subscription, your rules." *(friction)*
- "No repeats. No guesswork. Just new ways to catch." *(friction, outcome)*
- "More than a subscription. A reason to hit the water." *(outcome)*

Notice the pattern: the strongest ones lift a specific phrase the customer already said ("two
lures for eighty bucks", "another pack of plastics") rather than describing the product.

## Copywriting frameworks we lean on

When a formula doesn't fit, fall back to a structure:

- **PAS.** Problem, agitate, solution. Best for friction-led pages.
- **AIDA.** Attention, interest, desire, action. Best for a full narrative scroll.
- **Now you can.** State the outcome the customer couldn't reach before. Best for a hero line.
