# The "How We'd Approach [Brand]" Doc

The shorthand we send a prospect to show we get them, before there's a proposal or a price.

This is the front of the post-click sales motion. It is not a proposal and not a SOW. It is a short, human-feeling document that says "we looked at you, here is how we'd think about the work, here is what we'd need from your side to do it properly." It is what we send after a teardown or a pitch call to move a prospect deeper into the process. Graham works the pipeline and the polish; the post-click team (Ash, Lucas) writes the substance. The skill `brand-prospect-doc` produces this exact format.

The doc earns trust because it is honest about what NOT to do. Most CRO shops pitch every tactic they own. This doc names what is already working, names what is not worth doing for this specific brand, and only then says what the real work is. That is the differentiator on the page.

## The recipe

Always the same three sections, in this order. The opener is two sentences, no filler, no "hope this finds you well."

> A quick note on how we'd approach working with [Brand]. Not a full proposal, more a shorthand of how we're thinking about it, what we'd want to explore together, and what we'd need from your side to do it properly.

### Section 1: How we'd approach [Brand]
Three to five bullets, never more. Each bullet is a positioning statement about how we think about this kind of brand, not a list of tactics.

- **First bullet names what the brand does NOT need.** This is what sets us apart from every other CRO shop approaching them. Zebra: "isn't a brand that needs help converting. A 5.9% conversion rate over the last 30 days is genuinely strong." Heirloom: "isn't a brand that needs help refining its story."
- **Then name the real constraint.** Where the actual opportunity sits, in plain terms. Usually average order value (AOV), subscription or repeat-purchase economics, or a funnel gap, not raw conversion rate. Heirloom: "it functions as a catalog, not a funnel." Zebra: "low AOV and subscription sitting around 25-30% of monthly revenue."
- **Then name the kind of work.** Structural, not surface. "Offer architecture, subscription mechanics, and using landing pages as structured testing environments," not "section-by-section CRO."
- **Compliment once, genuinely, specific.** Brand world, fundamentals, product, audience, asset library. One short clause inside a bullet, not a sentence of praise. It signals we actually looked.

### Section 2: Potential ideas
Always split into three sub-sections, in this order. Intro line: "Split into things that need a conversation with whoever owns operations on your side and things we can build alongside without changing how [Brand] runs."

- **Operations-driven (bigger lifts).** The higher-impact moves that need the brand's buy-in to even attempt because they change how the brand runs: subscription as the default path, multi-unit bundles, tiered volume discounts, loyalty reframed as the entry point, starter kits, an offer system tested on landing pages then graduated to the core site.
- **Things we can tag onto without changing operations.** The moves that show value early without the brand changing anything: an ongoing landing-page testing programme, A/B tests on the core site (one to two at a time at lower traffic), product-level messaging, merchandising existing collections better, cart free-shipping nudges, a quiz as a guided-selling funnel, replacing sold-out dead-ends with email capture.
- **Things we'd recommend skipping.** The credibility section. Name what is not worth doing for this brand and tie each to a reason rooted in this brand. Common skips: heavy section-by-section CRO when the site already converts, exit-intent popups that clash with an anti-marketing brand, personalization or search engines without the traffic depth to justify them, aggressive A/B testing on low-traffic pages.

Each bullet is one to three sentences. Lead with the move, then the reason. The reader is smart, do not over-explain.

### Section 3: What we'd need from you
A short intro line ("To do this properly rather than guess, we'd want a conversation about:") then the data, context, or access that unlocks a proper roadmap. The list is the diagnostic kit for the first 90 days: subscription rate by product and channel, AOV split by first-time versus returning, which bundles are moving, ad spend and channel mix, subscribe-and-save economics, quiz completion rates, P&L and margin where the model needs it, Shopify admin access, and time with whoever owns fulfilment and operations.

Close on one line offering the roadmap, friendly not pushy:

> From there we can put together a proper roadmap with sequencing, ownership, and the bigger calls flagged honestly. Happy to chat through any of this whenever works.

## Voice rules that matter most
- "We'd" not "we will." Conditional, collaborative, positioning a partnership not delivering one.
- "You" not "the brand." They are reading it.
- Quote anything the brand owns the first time it appears: collection names ("The Ultimate Clean Routine"), program names ("Passport Rewards"), quiz names ("Routine Builder"), status tags. If the reader could think we made the word up, quote it.
- Spell out acronyms on first use: "average order value (AOV)", then AOV.
- Concrete over vague. "A cart progress bar" becomes "a cart progress bar (something like '$X away from free shipping')."
- No em dashes. No buzzwords (leverage, ecosystem, drive growth). No "I", it is "we" throughout.
- Total length around 700 words. If it starts feeling comprehensive, cut it. The gold standard is the Sundae School doc.

## Two variants of the same doc
The recipe above is the default. Two real variants exist for different situations:

- **Audit format.** When the brand has a large catalog and the opportunity is spread across page surfaces, the doc reads as a page-by-page CRO audit instead. Green Garden's version is titled "Website CRO Audit & Recommendations" and walks Findability and Navigation, Search, Offers, Homepage, Collection pages, product pages, Merchandising, Design, Landing pages, then closes with "The Opportunity" and "Where We'd Focus" (the workstreams). Use this when the brand wants to see the specific site problems, often as the lever Graham uses to pull a prospect deeper into the sales process.
- **Two-route project proposal.** When the brand needs a build or replatform rather than a retainer, the doc offers two routes at two prices built on the same foundation. Bovita's version opens with "Where [Brand] is right now," then Option A (Straightforward Migration) and Option B (Full Discovery and Build), each with Scope of work / What you get at the end / What this is not / Timeframe / Best fit if. It closes with "How to choose between them" and "How we'd kick this off." This one carries pricing, so it sits closer to a SOW than a shorthand doc.

The default shorthand doc carries no price. The moment a doc carries scope plus a number, it has crossed into SOW territory (see `sow-playbook.md`). Ash's own line on the split: Green Garden was "a SoW with pricing," Zebra and Heirloom were "heuristics" (how-we'd-approach docs).

## Where it lives and how it ships
Default destination is the Internal Docs Notion database (`2a6b4e6a-dca1-8102-861f-dbe3e0491b99`), Type "New Business", titled "How we'd approach [Brand]". The team writes the substance; Graham makes it look pretty and sends it, often bundled with a quiz demo and one or two case studies. An intro call is the default precursor but not mandatory: if the prospect has already been pitched, the doc can follow without a call.
