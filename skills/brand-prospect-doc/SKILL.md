# Brand Prospect Doc

Turns strategic thinking already worked through in conversation into a short, human-feeling Notion document the user can send to a brand prospect. The output is deliberately shorthand: not a proposal, not a report, just a clear summary of how we'd approach them, what ideas are on the table, and what we'd need from their side to go further.

The user's mental model: they've done the thinking already (probably in this conversation, sometimes in a previous one). They want to send the prospect something that signals "we get you, here's how we'd think about it, here's what we'd need." It should read like a smart person typed it after a meeting, not like an AI report. If the user has already developed the strategic thinking in conversation, lean on that and don't repeat the research. If they haven't, ask or research before writing.

---

## The three-section recipe

Always uses this structure. Don't deviate. The reason it works is that it mirrors how Ash actually thinks about a prospect: framing first, ideas split by what's actually moveable, and an honest ask for what's needed to go further.

### Section 1: How we'd approach [Brand]

Three to five bullets, never more. Each bullet is a *positioning statement* about how the agency thinks about this kind of brand, not a list of tactics. The first bullet usually names what the brand *doesn't* need (e.g. "isn't a brand that needs traditional CRO") because that's what differentiates the agency's pitch from every other CRO shop they're being approached by. Subsequent bullets explain what the real work is, why operations matters, and what the working mindset is.

Compliment the brand genuinely once or twice in this section. Brand world, fundamentals, product, audience: find the real strength and name it. This is not flattery, it's calibration: it signals we've actually looked at them and understand what's already good. Don't be effusive. One short clause inside a bullet, not a whole sentence of praise.

### Section 2: Potential ideas

Always split into three sub-sections in this order:

**Operations-driven (bigger lifts).** Things that require a conversation with whoever owns ops/stock/margin/inventory at the prospect. These are the higher-impact moves but they need their buy-in to even attempt. Examples: defining an evergreen core capsule, bundle pricing, stock-honest scarcity, repeat-purchase architecture, anything that changes how the brand actually operates.

**Things we can tag onto without changing operations.** Things the agency can do without the brand changing how they run. These are the moves that show value early. Examples: fixing dead-end pages, surfacing existing collections better, free shipping mechanics, LP templates, PDP improvements, editorial cross-linking, cart drawer upsells.

**Things we'd recommend skipping.** This is the section that makes the doc feel honest. Most agencies pitch every possible tactic. This one names what's not worth doing for this specific brand. Examples for fashion: aggressive A/B testing on tiny sample sizes, exit-intent popups, internal search overhauls for small catalogs, personalization engines without data depth. Keep this short, two to four bullets, and tie each to *why* it's wrong for this brand specifically.

Each bullet across all three sub-sections should be one to three sentences. Lead with the move, then the reason it works (or doesn't). Don't over-explain. The reader is smart, they don't need the agency to spell every implication out.

### Section 3: What we'd need from you

A short intro line ("To do this properly rather than guess, we'd want a conversation about:") followed by a bulleted list of the data, context, or access that would unlock the proper roadmap. Common items: drop cadence and sell-through, repeat purchase rate, return rate by category, margin by category, evergreen SKU candidates, list size and paid social spend, time with whoever owns ops.

Always close with a single line offering to put together a proper roadmap once they've shared what's needed. Friendly, not pushy. Something like "From there we can put together a proper roadmap with sequencing, ownership, and the bigger calls flagged honestly. Happy to chat through any of this whenever works."

## Clarity: don't let the reader guess what we mean

The doc gets read bullet by bullet, often by multiple people on the prospect's side. Every phrase has to be unambiguous on first read or the reader has to stop and work out whether something is the brand's language or ours, what an acronym means, or what a vague reference points to. That friction kills the doc.

Apply all of the following before posting.

**Quote anything the brand owns.** If a word or phrase is the prospect's own language (collection names, product names, community labels, internal terms, named drops, hero pieces), wrap it in quotation marks the first time it appears. This signals to the reader "this is your word, not ours" and removes the guessing game.

Examples of what to quote:
- Collection names ("Coordinated Sets", "New Arrivals")
- Internal community / member labels ("honor rollers", "founding members", "VIPs")
- Named drops or seasons (HIBERNATION'26, SPRING'26 can stay in their natural casing without quotes if the brand consistently uses uppercase, but flag them as brand language some other way like preserving their formatting)
- Editorial / content surfaces ("Textbook", "Field Notes", "The Journal")
- Named pages, manifestos, or lore ("10 Commandments", "Our Story", "Why we exist")
- Specific product names when referenced (IVORY TIE GORUM SHIRT, BLACK DOUBLE DUTY BLAZER)
- Status tags or system labels they use on-site ("Selling Fast", "Back Soon", "Last Chance")
- Anything else that's clearly their term rather than industry-standard language

The test: if the reader could plausibly think Ash made the word up, quote it.

**Add a brief clarifier when a brand term isn't self-explanatory.** If a brand-owned term needs context, give one short clarifying phrase the first time it's used. Format: `"Textbook" (your editorial blog)`. Don't over-explain, don't define every term, just unlock the ones that won't be obvious to someone reading bullet-by-bullet. A founder reading the doc knows what their own terms mean, but the doc often gets forwarded to a CMO, an investor, or an agency comparison shortlist where the reader doesn't have that context.

**Use concrete examples for vague claims.** "Sold-out product pages are dead-ends" is a fine line, but it's stronger as "Sold-out product pages are dead-ends, the IVORY TIE GORUM SHIRT page is a current example." Specifics signal we've actually looked. Pick one example per claim, not three. Don't pad.

**Show, don't summarise, when describing mechanics.** Instead of "a cart progress bar", write "a cart progress bar (something like '$65 away from free shipping')". The reader sees exactly what we mean in one extra clause. Same applies to copy suggestions: give an example string when it sharpens the idea.

**Spell out acronyms on first use, abbreviate after.** Even if the reader works in ecommerce, don't assume fluency. Write "product page (PDP)" on first appearance, then PDP from there on. Same for AOV ("average order value"), CVR ("conversion rate"), LTV ("lifetime value"). If a section uses only one acronym once, just spell it out and skip the abbreviation entirely.

**Avoid stacked jargon.** A sentence like "lifts UPT by surfacing PDP cross-sells against AOV thresholds" is dense even for an operator. Break it into a sentence with one term per clause, or rewrite in plain English. The doc should read fast on first scan.

**Pronoun and noun discipline.** "It", "this", "they" are landmines. If a bullet says "it's currently buried", the reader has to scroll back to find out what "it" is. Repeat the noun: "the 'Coordinated Sets' collection is currently buried". Slightly more words, much less re-reading.

## Voice and style

This is the part most templates get wrong. The doc has to feel human or it's worse than not sending one.

- **"We'd" not "we will".** Conditional, collaborative, not a commitment. The doc is positioning a partnership, not delivering one.
- **"You" not "the brand".** Speaks to the prospect directly. They're reading it.
- **Compliment without flattering.** If the brand world is strong, say so. If the fundamentals are in good shape, say so. Don't gush, don't manufacture praise. The compliment lands because it's specific.
- **Be willing to call something a skip.** The doc gains credibility from the "skip" section because it proves we're not just selling. Keep it honest, keep it short, tie each skip to a specific reason rooted in this brand.
- **Warm but disciplined.** Not chirpy, not corporate, not consultant-speak. Read every sentence and ask: would Ash actually say this out loud to the founder? If no, rewrite.
- **No em dashes anywhere.** Use commas, full stops, or restructure the sentence. Ash specifically doesn't want them. Check the final output before posting.
- **No filler intros.** No "I hope this finds you well." The opening line is a quick framing of what the doc is. Two sentences max.
- **No buzzwords.** Avoid "leverage", "synergy", "ecosystem", "best-in-class", "unlock value", "drive growth". Plain English. The reader is a founder or operator, not a procurement manager.

Note on jargon: the clarity section above covers acronyms and brand language. Voice-wise, just make sure the doc reads fast on first scan and would survive being read aloud.

The doc title should be specific to the brand. "How we'd approach [Brand]" is the default and works well. Don't title it anything generic like "Prospect Brief" or "Strategy Doc".

## Process

### 1. Check what we already have

Before doing any research, check what's already in the conversation. If the user has been working through this prospect with Claude in the current session, running teardowns, discussing approach, debating ideas, that's the source material. Don't re-do the work, distill it.

If the conversation doesn't have enough context, search past chats for the brand name. The user may have discussed them before.

If neither has anything substantive, do a quick scan of the brand's site and recent press to ground the doc in reality. But don't go deep. This is a synthesis output, not a fresh teardown. If a proper teardown hasn't happened yet, tell the user: "Before I write the prospect doc, do you want me to do a quick teardown first? Otherwise this will be more general than it should be." Let them decide.

### 2. Run the intake brief

The doc only works if it carries Ash's actual read on the prospect, not just a generic synthesis of what's on their site. Before writing, run a short intake to capture the texture: concerns, instincts, ideas Ash specifically wants to push, no-go zones, audience.

Frame it casually. Not a form, a chat. Something like:

> Before I write this, a few quick things to make sure your thinking is in here rather than just my read of their site. Five quick questions, mostly open, just type whatever comes to mind:

Then ask the five questions below. Ask them as a numbered list in one message so the user can answer in one go if they want, or break them up if they prefer. Don't pepper them one at a time, that gets annoying fast.

**The five questions:**

1. **Audience and reader.** Who's going to read this, and is there anything I should know about how they think? (Founder, marketing lead, ops lead, ecom manager, etc. If the user already said in conversation, skip this one.)

2. **Your read on them.** What's your honest take on the brand? What's actually going on that wouldn't be obvious from looking at the site? (Founder dynamics, what's broken, what's surprisingly good, where they're stuck, anything political.)

3. **Ideas you want represented.** Are there specific moves or angles you want in the doc, even if they're half-formed? Drop them in any state, I'll shape them into the right section. (Or "use your judgement" is a fine answer.)

4. **Concerns or no-go zones.** Anything I should avoid saying, areas that are sensitive, or things you've already pitched them that I shouldn't re-pitch?

5. **Where you want to push hardest.** What's the one thing in this doc that has to land? The headline takeaway, the bit you most want them to nod at.

**How to use the answers:**

- **Audience** shapes how the ops asks in section 3 are framed. Founder = direct. Marketing lead = softer ("whoever owns ops on your side"). Ops lead = can ask directly and in detail.
- **Your read** is the most important input. It feeds the *opener* of section 1 (the "isn't a brand that needs X" line) and the texture across all three sections. If the user gives you genuine insight here, weight it heavily over what's on the brand's website.
- **Ideas to represent** go into section 2. Sort them into ops-driven, tag-on, or skip based on what they require. Don't dilute them, sharpen them, but keep the user's framing intact. If they say something rough like "I think there's an AOV play in the sets" you write the section 2 bullet around that, you don't generalise it into "AOV optimisation opportunities."
- **Concerns / no-go zones** are hard constraints. Don't violate them even if the doc would be stronger.
- **Headline takeaway** is the thing the doc has to leave the reader with. If you can't trace a clear thread from the opening of section 1 through to section 3 that lands this point, rewrite until you can.

If the user has already given you most of this context earlier in the conversation, don't re-ask. Just check the gaps: "I've got X and Y from earlier, just need to check on Z and the audience before I write this."

### 3. Write the doc

Use the three-section recipe above. Render it as a markdown artifact / file in chat first so the user can read and approve before it goes anywhere.

Don't include any preamble outside the doc itself. No "Here's the draft" or "I've written this in a friendly tone". Just produce the doc.

### 4. After the doc, ask where it goes

End with a single short question:

> Where would you like this to live? (Notion in Internal Docs, or somewhere else?)

The default destination is Notion in the Internal Docs database. The user may want it elsewhere occasionally (Google Doc, email draft, Slack DM). Wait for their answer.

### 5. Route it

- **Notion (default).** Use `Notion:notion-create-pages` with parent set to the Internal Docs database (`database_id: 2a6b4e6a-dca1-8102-861f-dbe3e0491b99`). Title format: "How we'd approach [Brand]". Confirm with the resulting URL.
- **Slack draft.** Use `Slack:slack_send_message_draft`. Ask which channel.
- **Just show me / copy.** Do nothing further, the doc is in chat.
- **Other.** Confirm it's ready to copy and offer to reformat for the destination (email subject line, etc.).

### 6. Final pass before posting

Before sending the final markdown to any destination, do a clarity and style sweep. This is non-negotiable. Don't skip it.

**Clarity sweep:**

1. Scan every bullet for brand-owned language. Anything the prospect uses as their own term should be in quotation marks the first time it appears, with a brief clarifier if the term isn't self-explanatory. If you find an unquoted term that's clearly theirs, fix it.
2. Scan for acronyms. Any acronym used without being spelled out at least once gets spelled out on first use.
3. Scan for vague references: "it", "this", "they", "that one", "the page", "the collection". If the reader has to scroll back to figure out what's being referenced, replace the pronoun with the noun.
4. Scan for hand-wavy claims. Any claim that could be made concrete with one specific example should be. "Sold-out pages are dead-ends" gets a named example. "A cart progress bar" gets an example string.
5. Read every bullet aloud in your head. If you'd stumble or have to re-read, rewrite.

**Style sweep:**

6. Scan for em dashes (—) and en dashes (–). Replace each one. The fix is usually a comma (joining related clauses), a full stop (separating complete thoughts), "or" / "and" as a connector, or restructure the sentence if no clean punctuation swap works. This is non-negotiable. Ash has flagged em dashes specifically as an AI tell.
7. Scan for buzzwords from the blacklist (leverage, synergy, ecosystem, best-in-class, unlock value, drive growth). Replace with plain English.
8. Scan for "I" pronouns. The doc is "we" throughout.

If anything gets caught, fix silently and continue. Don't tell the user about the fixes unless they ask.

## Reference: the Sundae School doc as the gold standard

The doc this skill was built from is the template. If in doubt, mirror its structure, length, and voice. Key features:

- Opens in two sentences. Names what the doc *is* and what it isn't ("Not a full proposal, more a shorthand").
- The first bullet of section 1 names what the brand *doesn't* need.
- Section 1 has four bullets. Each is a positioning statement, not a tactic.
- Section 2 splits into ops-driven (five bullets), tag-on (eight bullets), skip (three bullets). Numbers vary by brand, but the proportions are right: tag-on is the longest, skip is the shortest but punchy.
- Section 3 is seven bullets of asks plus a one-line close offering to put a roadmap together.
- Total length: ~700 words. Don't go much longer.
- No em dashes anywhere in the final version.
- Every brand-owned term ("Coordinated Sets", "honor rollers", "Textbook", "10 Commandments", "Last Chance", named drops, named products) is quoted on first use, with a brief clarifier if needed.
- Vague claims have concrete examples attached (the dead-end product page names a specific SKU, the cart progress bar shows an example string).

## What to avoid

- Don't write a full proposal. This is a shorthand doc, not a pitch deck. If it starts feeling comprehensive, cut it.
- Don't list every possible CRO tactic in the "ideas" section. Pick the ones that fit this brand specifically. Generic = dead on arrival.
- Don't skip the "skip" section. It's the most credibility-building part of the doc.
- Don't manufacture compliments. If the brand fundamentals are weak, don't claim they're strong. Find what's actually good (product, audience, brand story, founder, niche) and name that instead.
- Don't auto-route to Notion. Always show the doc in chat first, get a nod, then route.
- Don't use the word "leverage". Or "ecosystem". Or "drive growth". The doc dies on contact with consultant-speak.
- Don't pad section 3 with asks you don't actually need. Each item should be something that would genuinely change what we'd recommend.
- Don't skip the intake brief. A doc written without the user's read on the prospect is a doc built from website scraping, and it shows. Even if the conversation seems to have enough context, do a quick gap-check before writing. The five-question intake is what makes the output feel like the user's thinking, not generic synthesis.
- Don't write "I" anywhere in the doc. It's "we" throughout. The doc represents the agency, not Ash personally.
- Don't include caveats inside the doc ("of course, this depends on your goals"). The doc is confident. Caveats go into the conversation, not the doc.
- Don't include the em dash check as a visible step in the conversation. Just do it silently before posting.

## Example structure (Sundae School, for reference)

```
A quick note on how we'd approach working with [Brand]. Not a full proposal, more a 
shorthand of how we're thinking about it, what we'd want to explore together, and what 
we'd need from your side to do it properly.

## How we'd approach [Brand]

- [Brand] isn't a brand that needs [thing they don't need]. [Specific compliment about 
  what's already strong.]
- That means the work isn't about [X]. It's about [Y], [Z], and [W].
- We'd want to work closely with whoever owns [operations / the relevant function] on 
  your side. [Why that matters for this brand specifically.]
- The mindset is structural: [one-line description of how we'd think about it].

## Potential ideas

Split into things that need an [ops/relevant function] conversation and things we can 
do alongside without changing how [Brand] runs.

**[Ops/Relevant function]-driven (bigger lifts)**

- [Idea]. [One-line reason it works for this brand.]
- ...

**Things we can tag onto without changing [operations]**

- [Idea]. [One-line reason it works.]
- ...

**Things we'd recommend skipping**

- [Skip]. [Why it's not right for this brand specifically.]
- ...

## What we'd need from you

To do this properly rather than guess, we'd want a conversation about:

- [Data point or context we'd need]
- [Another]
- [Another]
- Time with whoever owns [ops / the relevant function] on your side

From there we can put together a proper roadmap with sequencing, ownership, and the 
bigger calls flagged honestly. Happy to chat through any of this whenever works.
```
